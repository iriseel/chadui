The first time I performed agricultural labor of any kind was last year, the fall of 2021, shortly after coming to Yale.

I had just turned 25.

It took place at the Yale Farm, located at 345 Edwards Street, New Haven, and a 20-minute walk from my home.

I was there for only three hours.

We thinned beets, picked tomatoes, and fed chickens.

It was an odd feeling, being there. I kept thinking about my mother and her experience being sent “up to the mountains and down to the villages” in China in the 1970s.

As I massaged the beet greens in my hands, I wondered if any of my bodily motions overlapped with hers, decades ago and younger than me.

My strongest memory from that September day was that feeling of suspense,

a hesitant opening up of my self for a déjà vu that did not surface,

my body in a place while my mind was seeking memories elsewhere.
